## Executive Summary

A strong week for drought research, led by a landmark Nature study showing that atmospheric evaporative demand has amplified global drought severity by 40% and expanded drought-affected areas by 74% over the last five years. Complementary work in Nature Climate Change reveals that the dynamic predictability of agricultural drought is decreasing under warming, complicating early warning efforts. ## Global Drought Dynamics and Prediction

This week's drought literature reveals a troubling convergence: droughts are intensifying globally while becoming harder to predict. Gebrechorkos et al. present comprehensive evidence in Nature that atmospheric evaporative demand (AED) has driven a 40% increase in global drought severity since 1901, with drought-affected areas expanding by 74% in just the last five years. At the river-basin scale, Feng et al. apply a novel 3D clustering framework to track drought events across 59 global basins, revealing distinct spatial footprints and temporal changes in drought behavior. Meanwhile, Wu et al. demonstrate in Nature Climate Change that the dynamic predictability of agricultural drought is declining under warming conditions, posing significant challenges for early warning systems. Zhang et al. contribute an important methodological resource with CHM_Drought, a unified high-resolution multi-index drought dataset for mainland China spanning six decades.

### Warming accelerates global drought severity

**Authors**: S. Gebrechorkos, Justin Sheffield, S. Vicente‐Serrano et al.

**Journal**: *Nature* · **DOI**: [10.1038/s41586-025-09047-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09047-2) · **Citations**: 163

**Matched topics**: drought
{: .label .label-green }

> Drought is one of the most common and complex natural hazards affecting the environment, economies and populations globally. However, there are significant uncertainties in global drought trends, and a limited understanding of the extent to which a key driver, atmospheric evaporative demand (AED), impacts the recent evolution of the magnitude, frequency, duration and areal extent of droughts. Here, by developing an ensemble of high-resolution global drought datasets for 1901–2022, the authors find an increasing trend in drought severity worldwide. AED has increased drought severity by an average of 40% globally. Not only are typically dry regions becoming drier but also wet areas are experiencing drying trends. During the past 5 years (2018–2022), the areas in drought have expanded by 74% on average compared with 1981–2017, with AED contributing to 58% of this increase.

## Flood Risk, Exposure, and Climate Change

Three high-profile studies examine flood hazards from complementary angles. Rogers et al. use a high-resolution global flood model in Nature Communications to disentangle the drivers of future flood exposure, finding that population growth accounts for 77% of the projected increase by 2100 while climate change contributes 21%—a result that underscores the importance of land-use planning alongside climate adaptation. Sattar et al. provide a definitive reconstruction in Science of the October 2023 Sikkim GLOF cascade, documenting how a 14.7 million m³ moraine collapse triggered a chain of events that eroded 270 million m³ of sediment and devastated hydropower infrastructure along the Teesta River. At the regional scale, Haslinger et al. present robust evidence in Nature that increasing hourly heavy rainfall intensities in Austria are translating into measurable changes in flood regimes.

### The role of climate and population change in global flood exposure and vulnerability

**Authors**: Justin S Rogers, Marco P. Maneta, Stephan R. Sain et al.

**Journal**: *Nature Communications* · **DOI**: [10.1038/s41467-025-56654-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-56654-8) · **Citations**: 88

**Matched topics**: flood
{: .label .label-green }

> Flooding is a pervasive natural hazard with wide-ranging impacts on society. Using a high-resolution global flood model considering coastal, fluvial, and pluvial hazards, this study clarifies the role of climate effects versus population growth effects in changing flood exposure. Between 2020 and 2100, the population likely exposed to 1% annual risk (100-year) flood hazard will increase from 1.6 to 1.9 billion people. Of this change, 21.1% is attributed to climate change, 76.8% to population change, and 2.1% to both. The largest driver of uncertainty in exposure is population change, while climate change remains a smaller but still important driver.

### The Sikkim flood of October 2023: Drivers, causes and impacts of a multihazard cascade

**Authors**: A. Sattar, Kristen L. Cook, Shashi Kant Rai, Etienne Berthier, Simon Allen, Sonam Rinzin et al.

**Journal**: *Science* · **DOI**: [10.1126/science.ads2659](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.ads2659) · **Citations**: 73

**Matched topics**: flood
{: .label .label-green }

> On 3 October 2023, a multihazard cascade in the Sikkim Himalaya, India, was triggered by 14.7 million m³ of frozen lateral moraine collapsing into South Lhonak Lake, generating an ~20 m tsunami-like impact wave, breaching the moraine, and draining ~50 million m³ of water. The ensuing Glacial Lake Outburst Flood (GLOF) eroded ~270 million m³ of sediment, which overwhelmed infrastructure, including hydropower installations along the Teesta River. The physical scale and human and economic impact of this event prompts urgent reflection on the role of climate change and human activities in exacerbating such disasters.

## Flood and Runoff Modeling with Machine Learning

Data-driven approaches continue to advance flood and runoff prediction. Mahakur et al. combine spatial interpolation techniques with deep learning to predict runoff at ungauged catchments, addressing one of the most persistent challenges in operational hydrology. Islam et al. provide a comprehensive systematic review of urban flood susceptibility mapping methods, comparing remote sensing, machine learning, and traditional modeling approaches. Two additional studies apply GIS-based and machine learning models—Ashfaq et al. using analytical hierarchy process and frequency ratio, and Khodaei et al. employing XGBoost, Random Forest, and SVM—to map flood susceptibility under current and future climate and land-use scenarios.

## Surface Water Resources and Hydrological Data

Research on surface water storage, availability, and monitoring rounds out this week's literature. Zhao et al. report in Nature Water that surface water storage in global drylands has become decoupled from precipitation patterns due to anthropogenic activity, a finding with major implications for water resource planning in arid regions. Li et al. contribute a valuable new global dataset of hydrogen and oxygen stable isotopes for surface water, published in Earth System Science Data, enabling improved tracing of water cycle processes worldwide. Borah examines the implications of climate change for urban water supply systems, highlighting the growing challenge of water stress in cities.
