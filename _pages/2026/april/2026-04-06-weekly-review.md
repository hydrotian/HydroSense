## Reservoir and Water-Energy Systems

Coupled water-energy management under extreme events emerged as a key focus this week. Guo et al. developed a hierarchical emergency scheduling strategy that coordinates flexibility resources across electricity and watershed networks during drought, demonstrating significant reductions in both power shortages and water curtailments on a realistic 143-node distribution network. Meanwhile, Liu et al. quantified the often-overlooked evaporation losses from reservoir development on the Loess Plateau, finding that expansion of small- and medium-sized reservoirs has driven substantial increases in total evaporation despite a slight decrease in evaporation rates from climate effects alone.

### Hierarchical Aggregation-Embedded Emergency Scheduling of Coupled Electricity-Watershed Networks With Heterogeneous Flexibility Resources Under Extreme Drought Events

**Authors**: Siyuan Guo, Bin Zhou, C. Chung, Siqi Bu, Zhihao Hua, Jun Liu

**Journal**: *IEEE Transactions on Sustainable Energy* · **DOI**: [10.1109/TSTE.2025.3635922](https://doi.org/10.1109/TSTE.2025.3635922) · **Citations**: 6

**Matched topics**: drought
{: .label .label-green }

> This paper proposes a dynamic aggregation-embedded emergency scheduling strategy to coordinate massive heterogeneous flexibility resources of electricity and watershed networks for mitigating power shortages under drought events. A thermodynamic model of coupled electricity-watershed networks is formulated to express risk interdependencies of power shortage and water curtailments under high-temperature climates. A hypercube multi-directional inner approximation projection method evaluates variable water-dependent power dispatchable regions. The proposed strategy has been fully tested on a practical 143-node distribution network coupled with a 14-junction watershed system.

### Increased Surface Water Evaporation Loss Induced by Reservoir Development on the Loess Plateau

**Authors**: Yao Liu, Xianhong Xie, Yibing Wang, Arken Tursun, Dawei Peng, Xinran Wu et al.

**Journal**: *Hydrology and Earth System Sciences* · **DOI**: [10.5194/hess-30-67-2026](https://doi.org/10.5194/hess-30-67-2026) · **Citations**: 1

**Matched topics**: reservoir, surface water
{: .label .label-green }

> Global-scale reservoir construction has significantly enhanced water supply for local production and livelihoods, yet evaporation losses from these surface water bodies remain poorly understood. Using a modified Penman model and long-term remote sensing water body data for the Loess Plateau (2000–2018), the authors find that total evaporation volume amounts to 4.16 × 10⁶ m³ d⁻¹ with an upward trend. The development of small- and medium-sized reservoirs and check dams is the primary driver of increased evaporation losses, which are comparable to surface water withdrawals in this region.

## Climate Change Impacts on Water Resources

Climate adaptation and its hydrological consequences drew significant attention this week. Taylor provides compelling global evidence that farmers have responded to warming by expanding groundwater irrigation, with observed warming responsible for 9% of global irrigation growth—but at the cost of aquifer stress and soil salinity. In mountainous watersheds, Casirati et al. used SWAT+ to show that large-scale forest restoration in California's Kings River basin can offset up to 48% of warming-driven streamflow declines, while Gorsevski applied TOPMODEL with CLIGEN to project streamflow sensitivity in data-sparse Mediterranean settings. The Valencia flash flood study by Calvo-Sancho et al. in Nature Communications provides a timely reminder that climate change is amplifying extreme precipitation events with devastating consequences.

### Irrigation and Climate Change: Long-run Adaptation and its Externalities

**Authors**: Charles A. Taylor

**Journal**: *Journal of the Association of Environmental and Resource Economists* · **DOI**: [10.1086/741141](https://doi.org/10.1086/741141) · **Citations**: 5

**Matched topics**: climate change, irrigation
{: .label .label-green }

> As the largest user of global water, irrigated agriculture accounts for 20% of global cropland and 40% of food production. This paper finds that farmers adapt to climate change by increasing groundwater irrigation in places that became drier and hotter. Observed recent warming is responsible for 9% of global irrigation growth. GRACE satellite data shows that climate-driven irrigation contributes to aquifer stress and increased soil salinity, representing large negative externalities of adaptation and a potential threat to future food security.

### Hydrological Response to Compounding Impacts of Climate Change and Forest Management in the Upper Kings River Basin, CA, USA

**Authors**: Stefano Casirati, Martha Conklin, M. Safeeq

**Journal**: *Ecohydrology* · **DOI**: [10.1002/eco.70157](https://doi.org/10.1002/eco.70157) · **Citations**: 1

**Matched topics**: river, climate change
{: .label .label-green }

> Using the SWAT+ model, the authors investigated individual and compounding effects of forest treatments on evapotranspiration and streamflow in the upper Kings River basin under warming scenarios. Large-scale forest restoration has the potential to partially mitigate warming impacts on streamflow by up to 48% and 36% under +1.5 °C and +3.0 °C warming, respectively. Benefits are most prominent in the first year following treatment and persist up to 10 years.

## Flood Research and Compound Events

Flood hazard assessment methods and drought-flood compound events featured prominently. Angkanasirikul et al. analyzed 70 tropical cyclone events over 45 years in northern Vietnam, developing a systematic flood susceptibility mapping methodology that links TC characteristics with catchment-specific hydrological drivers. Li et al. examined the spatiotemporal variability of drought-flood abrupt alternation events in Southwest China over six decades, highlighting the increasing frequency of these compound extremes.

### Key Factors Influencing Flood Severity and Mapping of Flood Susceptibility in Northern Vietnam and Its Red River Delta From Landfalling Tropical Cyclones

**Authors**: Warinthorn Angkanasirikul, Wei Jian, Edmond Yatman Lo

**Journal**: *Hydrological Processes* · **DOI**: [10.1002/hyp.70372](https://doi.org/10.1002/hyp.70372) · **Citations**: 1

**Matched topics**: river, flood
{: .label .label-green }

> Using data covering 70 tropical cyclone events from 1979 to 2023, the authors investigated interactions between TC characteristics, catchment properties, and observed flood responses in Northern Vietnam. Severity metrics correlate with TC properties (particularly average daily precipitation) and catchment-specific characteristics (elevation, runoff coefficient, antecedent soil saturation). Flood susceptibility maps were generated via AHP combined with fuzzy comprehensive evaluation and benchmarked against historical flood extent data.

## Machine Learning for Runoff Prediction

Two independent studies advanced ML architectures for daily runoff forecasting. Yang et al. developed a two-stage LSTM approach that integrates the Xin'anjiang conceptual model with ensemble empirical mode decomposition, combining process-based understanding with deep learning. Xu et al. introduced an innovative architecture coupling the black-winged kite optimization algorithm with a Mamba2-Transformer model for daily runoff prediction, exploring newer attention-based architectures beyond standard LSTMs.

## Streamflow Modeling and Monitoring

Zipper et al. addressed a practical gap in water management by incorporating stream drying into analytical depletion functions for estimating streamflow impacts from groundwater pumping. Their approach reveals that summer stream drying causes substantial lagged streamflow depletion into fall/winter when networks rewet—a critical insight for seasonal water management. Patel et al. provided a systematic review of advances in remote sensing and GIS applications across watershed hydrology, mapping the evolution of data-driven tools in the field.

### Lagged Streamflow Depletion Due to Pumping-Induced Stream Drying: Incorporation Into Analytical Streamflow Depletion Estimation Methods

**Authors**: S. Zipper, Ian Gambill, Monty Schmitt, Claire Kouba, Leland Scantlebury, Thomas Harter et al.

**Journal**: *Journal of Hydrology* · **DOI**: [10.1016/j.jhydrol.2026.134909](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jhydrol.2026.134909) · **Citations**: 1

**Matched topics**: hydrologic model, streamflow
{: .label .label-green }

> Water management often requires accounting for reductions in streamflow caused by groundwater pumping. The authors develop an approach to incorporate stream drying into analytical depletion functions (ADFs). Using Scott Valley, California as an example, ADFs incorporating stream drying show strong agreement with observed streamflow and process-based numerical model results. Critically, ADFs with drying can simulate a temporal shift in streamflow depletion that occurs when summer stream drying causes network disconnections and a substantial fraction of depletion is lagged until the fall/winter rewetting period.
